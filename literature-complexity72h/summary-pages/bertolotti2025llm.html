Introduction

The paper proposes an LLM-based version of the Delphi method for qualitative forecasting. The Delphi method is normally based on repeated rounds of expert opinions and feedback. The authors argue that this is useful when systems are complex, fast-changing, and hard to model with data. The proposed method replaces human experts with LLM agents that simulate different expert perspectives. The case study focuses on the future evolution of Generative AI. The paper has three aims: propose a forecasting framework, explore it as a reasoning architecture, and generate insights about GenAI. The authors do not claim that LLMs are equivalent to human experts. Instead, they test whether LLMs can support structured foresight in data-scarce domains. The study is presented as preliminary and exploratory.

Methods

The methodology follows the structure of a classical Delphi study. There are two types of agents: an organizing agent and responding agents. The organizing agent plays the role of facilitator: it collects answers, reformulates them, and generates new questions. The responding agents simulate experts and answer open-ended or closed-ended questions. The same LLM is used for both types of agents, but with different system prompts and action spaces. The whole Delphi process is described as a stochastic function. It receives a topic, a set of roles, and initial questions, then returns a structured prediction. The process is iterative and designed to generate diverse perspectives over multiple rounds.

Problem Definition

The method is designed for problems where data-driven forecasting is not reliable or possible. It is also useful when causal models cannot be built because the system is too complex or unstable. The authors emphasize systems whose internal mechanisms change over time. In such cases, past data may not be useful for predicting future trajectories. The method is appropriate when multiple perspectives are needed. It is not meant for precise numerical forecasting. Its goal is to generate qualitative insight and structured scenario exploration. The selected case, GenAI evolution, is relevant, complex, fast-changing, and difficult to model formally.

Expert Definition

In traditional Delphi studies, researchers select human experts. In this LLM-based version, the “experts” are simulated by assigning roles to LLM agents. Each agent is defined by nationality, education, professional experience, field of experience, and specialization. These role features are randomly sampled at the beginning of the simulation. The goal is to create heterogeneity among respondents. The role information is passed through the system prompt. No additional behavioral customization is used. This design allows both reproducibility and stochastic variation. The authors recognize that prompt-based role assignment may not fully overcome model-level bias.

Question Design and Iteration

The first open-ended questions are manually generated by the authors. Open-ended questions are used to maximize the generation of new ideas. The responding agents are asked to provide short answers to reduce computational cost. The organizing agent transforms open-ended answers into closed-ended questions and later generates new open-ended questions. Closed-ended questions use a 1-to-5 scale to force clearer positions. The process runs for five rounds. The goal is not only consensus, but also exploration of new scenarios. To prevent question explosion, the authors filter generated questions using embeddings and cosine similarity. This preserves diversity while keeping the process computationally manageable.

Difference from Classical Delphi Methods

The LLM-based Delphi does not stop when consensus is reached. Instead, it stops after a fixed number of rounds. This choice reduces the need for human supervision and supports the interpretation of the method as a reasoning architecture. Later open-ended questions are generated from average responses, not mainly from controversial answers. The system generates redundant questions and then removes similar ones using semantic similarity. This reduces dependence on the LLM’s ability to process long texts. The method avoids human problems such as fatigue, dropout, and personality-driven bandwagon effects. However, it introduces new problems related to model bias, prompt sensitivity, and knowledge cutoffs.

Experimental Configuration and Implementation

The experiment uses GPT-4o-mini-2024-07-18 as the core LLM. The model is selected because it is accessible through OpenAI’s API and balances performance with cost. The temperature is set to 0.7 to preserve stochastic behavior. The authors repeat simulations three times to assess variability. The implementation is written in Python 3.13.1. The system uses external APIs rather than local models. Parallel API calls are used to reduce execution time. The method does not require strong local hardware because the main computation is handled by the API provider. The full system is made available through an online repository.

Discussion: Generated Results

The generated results emphasize governance, collaboration, inequality, and measurement. A recurring idea is that GenAI impacts are interdependent across technology, regulation, economy, and society. The outputs focus more on sociological and governance aspects than on technical details. The discussion highlights the need for shared infrastructure and exchange platforms. The authors also observe a positive bias toward GenAI. Risks and critical perspectives appear, but they are not central. Topics such as existential risk, consciousness, and sentience are almost absent. This may reflect the initial questions, model bias, or the structure of the process. Future versions should deliberately include more critical and minority perspectives.

Pros

  • The paper proposes an original method that adapts Delphi studies to LLM-based multi-agent systems.
  • The method is useful for complex, data-scarce, and rapidly evolving domains where quantitative forecasting is weak.
  • The framework reduces human-specific problems such as fatigue, dropout, and social pressure effects.
  • The repeated stochastic setup allows analysis of variability and sensitivity, which is difficult in classical Delphi studies.
  • The GenAI case study produces a broad and structured map of relevant future issues, especially governance and inequality.

Cons

  • The method depends strongly on the base LLM, so simulated “experts” may not be truly heterogeneous.
  • Knowledge cutoff is a serious limitation for forecasting fast-changing domains such as GenAI.
  • The results may reflect prompt framing and model biases more than independent expert-like reasoning.
  • The generated GenAI scenarios are somewhat optimistic and underrepresent highly critical perspectives.
  • The study is preliminary and does not yet provide strong validation against human experts or real future outcomes.
