## Introduction

The paper introduces AgentSociety, a large-scale simulator for LLM-driven social agents.
Its goal is to support generative social science by creating artificial societies from the bottom up.
The authors argue that traditional social experiments are costly, difficult to scale, and sometimes ethically constrained.
Agent-based models are useful, but many existing agents are rule-based and too limited to reproduce realistic human behavior.
AgentSociety combines LLM-driven agents, a realistic societal environment, and a scalable simulation engine.
The system simulates more than 10,000 agents and around 5 million interactions.
It is tested on social phenomena such as polarization, inflammatory-message spread, universal basic income, hurricane response, and urban sustainability.
The paper presents AgentSociety as both a research platform for social scientists and a policy testbed for decision-makers.
Its central claim is that LLM agents can help move computational social science toward more realistic generative simulation.

## AgentSociety: Design and Overview

AgentSociety is built around three main components.
The first component is a population of LLM-driven social generative agents.
The second component is a realistic societal environment with urban, social, and economic spaces.
The third component is a large-scale simulation engine for distributed execution and communication.
The evaluation framework on page 5 compares LLM social simulators across minds, behaviors, mind-behavior coupling, environment, scale, and social-science tools.
AgentSociety is designed to simulate not only isolated decisions but full social lives.
It supports mobility, social interaction, employment, consumption, emotional updates, memory, surveys, interviews, and interventions.
Compared with many previous platforms, it aims to combine behavioral richness with large-scale execution.
The system is therefore positioned as a comprehensive artificial society rather than a narrow task simulator.

## LLM-Driven Social Generative Agents

The agents are designed as social beings with profiles, states, mental processes, memory, and behaviors.
The profile includes stable traits such as demographics and personality.
The dynamic status includes mental state, economic condition, and social relationships.
The mental process includes emotion, needs, and cognition.
The behavioral layer includes mobility, social interactions, employment, consumption, and simpler activities.
The diagram on page 7 shows how profile, mental process, behavior, memory, and environment interact.
The authors emphasize that human behavior is not generated by one module alone.
Instead, behavior emerges from the coupling of internal needs, emotional states, cognitive attitudes, memory, and environmental feedback.
This is the main design difference from simpler role-play agents.

## Emotion, Needs, and Cognition

The agent’s emotional state is updated after internal and external events.
Emotion is represented through keywords, sentence-level thoughts, and intensities for sadness, joy, fear, disgust, anger, and surprise.
Needs are inspired by Maslow’s hierarchy and act as motivational drivers.
Needs are updated according to actions, passive events, and current psychological states.
Cognition includes attitudes, thoughts, reasoning, and world understanding.
Attitudes toward topics are represented on a 0–10 scale and updated through experience.
The diagram on page 8 shows emotion, needs, cognition, behavior, and memory as an interacting loop.
This design allows agents to adapt their behavior over time rather than simply respond to prompts.
The paper’s aim is to make agents psychologically plausible enough for social simulation.

## Mobility Behaviors

Mobility connects agent needs to physical action in urban space.
If an agent is hungry, it may move to a restaurant.
If an agent has a work obligation, it may commute to an office.
The mobility module follows a need-plan-behavior sequence.
It first extracts mobility intention, then selects place type, decides a feasible radius, and chooses a specific place.
The final place selection uses a gravity model based on attractiveness and distance.
This reduces the computational load on the LLM while preserving human-like spatial behavior.
The diagram on page 10 shows how profile, status, map variables, weather, and gravity-model choice are combined.
Mobility also enables later social and economic behaviors, such as meetings, shopping, or work.

## Social Behaviors

Social behavior is modeled through relationships and communication.
The framework includes family bonds, friendships, and colleague relationships.
Each relationship has a strength value from 0 to 100.
Agents communicate more often with stronger relationships and adapt tone to relationship type.
For example, they use more formal language with colleagues and more casual language with friends.
Agents choose targets according to needs, relationship strength, and relevant expertise.
Message content depends on needs, thoughts, beliefs, and emotional state.
Received messages can change emotion, cognition, and future behavior.
The social module supports information diffusion, influence, relationship dynamics, and later offline meetings.

## Economic Behaviors

Economic behavior includes employment and consumption.
The paper models work propensity and consumption propensity as central behavioral variables.
Work propensity determines working hours and income.
Consumption propensity determines monthly spending budget and allocation decisions.
Economic decisions are affected by income, prices, taxes, savings, and previous consumption.
Agents interact with firms, banks, government, and markets.
Firms use labor to produce goods and adjust wages and prices according to demand.
Banks pay interest on savings, and government collects taxes.
The economic-space diagram on page 12 shows links among income, savings, consumption, firms, banks, and government.
The current model is useful but still simplifies labor markets and goods markets.

## Agent Workflow and Memory

The agent workflow is organized around stream memory.
Memory includes profile, status, and stream memory.
Profile stores stable information such as age and gender.
Status stores dynamic variables such as needs, satisfaction, and financial state.
Stream memory has event flow and perception flow.
Event flow records actions, passive events, and environmental changes over time.
Perception flow records the agent’s thoughts, attitudes, and emotional reactions to those events.
The workflow on page 13 shows how needs become plans and plans become mobility, social, economic, or other behaviors.
This structure creates a feedback loop between behavior, memory, emotion, cognition, and future action.

## Real-World Societal Environment

The societal environment is designed to reduce LLM hallucination and provide objective feedback.
It is divided into urban space, social space, and economic space.
The urban space supports roads, AOIs, POIs, walking, driving, buses, and taxis.
The social space supports online and offline interaction, social networks, message filtering, and user bans.
The economic space supports firms, agents, government, banks, taxation, consumption, savings, and macroeconomic indicators.
The diagram on page 14 shows how the three spaces map to mobility, social interaction, employment, and consumption.
The authors argue that LLMs should focus on subjective behavior while the environment handles objective constraints.
This separation is important because LLMs alone are weak at precise physical, economic, and numerical simulation.
The environment therefore acts as the realistic world in which agents act and receive feedback.

## Urban Space

The urban space models the physical city.
It includes road networks, lanes, junctions, AOIs, and POIs.
AOIs represent larger functional regions such as residential or commercial areas.
POIs represent specific destinations such as stores or restaurants.
The mobility layer supports driving, walking, buses, and taxis.
Driving uses acceleration and lane-changing models.
Pedestrians follow sidewalks and traffic signals.
Buses follow fixed schedules, while taxis use a dispatch system.
The urban data come from sources such as OpenStreetMap and SafeGraph.

## Social Space

The social space manages relationships and communication.
The social network is provided or loaded by the user.
Relationships and connection strengths can change during simulation.
Online social interaction is modeled through messages between agents.
Offline interaction can occur through spatial proximity or planned meetings.
The system includes a supervisor for social-media-like intervention.
The supervisor can filter messages, apply rules, and ban users or connections.
This makes the social space useful for studying information propagation and moderation.
It is especially important for experiments on inflammatory-message spread and polarization.

## Economic Space

The economic space models simplified macroeconomic flows.
Agents earn wages from firms, pay taxes, consume goods, and save money in banks.
Firms hire labor, produce output, pay wages, and adjust prices.
Government collects taxes and can redistribute resources.
Banks pay interest and adjust interest rates using a Taylor-rule-like mechanism.
The National Bureau of Statistics collects indicators such as GDP, working hours, and consumption.
This design links micro-level agent decisions with macroeconomic outcomes.
It enables experiments such as universal basic income.
However, the authors acknowledge that the current labor and goods markets are simplified.

## Large-Scale Social Simulation Engine

The engine treats each agent as an independent simulation unit.
Agents do not require a fixed execution order.
They exchange information through a messaging system.
The architecture uses Ray for distributed execution and asyncio for I/O concurrency.
Agents are grouped into agent groups to reduce communication overhead and reuse connections.
MQTT is used for high-throughput message transmission, inspired by IoT systems.
The system also includes LLM API adapters, retry mechanisms, JSON parsing, metrics, logs, and database storage.
The architecture diagram on page 18 shows shared services, Ray actors, agent groups, environment simulators, and GUI components.
The design is meant to scale social simulation beyond small multi-agent demonstrations.

## System Architecture and Services

The shared services include LLM APIs, an MQTT server, a database, and a metric recorder.
LLMs are the core cognitive component of the agents.
The MQTT server handles inter-agent communication.
PostgreSQL stores simulation results.
MLflow records metrics and supports experiment comparison.
Each experiment creates environment simulators and agent groups.
Agent groups execute agents in parallel using Ray actors.
This allows different experiments to share infrastructure while running separate simulations.
The architecture is practical because it uses mature open-source components rather than a fully custom stack.

## System Performance

The system-performance experiment tests scalability with 1,000 and 10,000 agents.
The simulator records LLM calls, input tokens, output tokens, total time, LLM time, and environment time.
The results show that increasing the number of groups reduces total time per round.
For 10,000 agents, total round time drops substantially when increasing groups from 8 to 32.
The environment time remains small, usually in the millisecond range.
The main bottleneck is LLM API latency.
The distribution plots on page 26 show input tokens, output tokens, LLM time, and environment time for 10,000 agents.
The authors conclude that the engine can support large-scale execution, but stable scaling beyond 10,000 agents may require private LLM deployment.
This is important because infrastructure, not agent logic alone, limits practical simulation scale.

## One-Day Life Experiment

The one-day life experiment shows how an agent performs daily activities.
It is used as a qualitative demonstration of self-directed social behavior.
The agent balances needs, emotional states, cognition, memory, and environmental constraints.
The experiment shows that agents can combine mobility, social behavior, economy, and cognition in one daily loop.
Table 5 reports about 491.68 daily environment interactions per agent.
Most interactions are urban-space get operations, while social messages average around 9 per day.
This indicates that simulated daily life involves many environment queries and fewer direct social messages.
The experiment helps demonstrate that AgentSociety is not only a social-media simulator.
It can represent broader daily routines embedded in city, economy, and social network.

## Polarization Experiment

The polarization experiment studies how opinions can become divided in a population.
The setup uses 100 agents, a social network, and a policy topic related to gun control.
Agents exchange messages and update their views through social interaction.
The goal is to test whether AgentSociety can reproduce opinion-distribution dynamics.
The experiment is connected to social-science methods such as survey and interview.
The authors use the simulation to observe how social influence can generate opinion clusters.
The case is meant to validate the simulator’s ability to reproduce polarization-like patterns.
It also shows how researchers can inspect mechanisms rather than only final outcomes.
The experiment is useful because polarization is a key macro-level phenomenon emerging from micro-level communication.

## Spread of Inflammatory Messages

The inflammatory-message experiment studies information propagation and emotional dynamics.
The setup uses 100 agents, a social network, and inflammatory messages.
Agents receive, transmit, or respond to messages through the social space.
The experiment tracks message spread and emotional responses.
The supervisor mechanism is relevant because it can filter or intervene in message propagation.
This case demonstrates how AgentSociety can simulate harmful content diffusion.
It also shows the platform’s relevance for moderation and intervention experiments.
The key idea is that inflammatory content affects both information flow and emotional states.
This makes it a useful test for the coupling between social behavior and mental processes.

## Universal Basic Income Experiment

The universal basic income experiment tests economic-policy intervention.
The setup uses 100 agents in Texas and cash distribution.
The simulation tracks economic and social metrics after the intervention.
The policy affects agent income, consumption, savings, and possibly work behavior.
This experiment demonstrates why a full social environment is needed.
A simple role-playing LLM sample could answer survey questions about UBI, but it could not simulate macro-level economic feedback.
AgentSociety can connect transfers to consumption, work propensity, social behavior, and aggregate indicators.
The case supports the paper’s claim that the platform can test complex policy designs.
However, the validity of this experiment depends strongly on the realism of the economic model.

## External Shock: Hurricane

The hurricane experiment tests response to an external environmental shock.
The setup uses 1,000 agents in Columbia and includes a weather-change event.
The simulation compares real and simulated mobility patterns around hurricane landfall.
The maps on page 34 show spatial activity before landfall, during landfall, and after landfall.
The normalized daily-trip plot shows a decline in activity around August 30 and recovery in early September.
The simulated trend broadly follows the real data, though deviations appear during the hurricane peak.
The authors interpret this as evidence that agents can approximate human mobility response to extreme weather.
This case is important because it validates behavior against real external-shock data.
It also suggests possible use for disaster preparedness and response planning.

## Urban Sustainability Experiment

The urban sustainability experiment tests pro-environmental interventions in Beijing.
The simulated city uses GIS data with AOIs and POIs.
The population includes 200 agents sampled according to 2024 Beijing census demographics.
Six independent research teams design eco-normative systems to encourage low-carbon travel.
Teams can use direct communication, poster placement, and city-wide announcements.
Poster placement and announcements have costs, and each team has a fixed budget.
The experiment lasts two simulated days.
Interventions are injected on the first day and evaluated on the second day.
This case shows how AgentSociety can act as a sandbox for comparing behavioral-policy strategies under resource constraints.

## Discussion: Social Simulator Levels

The paper situates AgentSociety within a broader view of social simulators.
The first level mirrors real-world behavior and detects social patterns.
The second level predicts future changes and supports intervention experiments.
The third level integrates simulation with real society in a hybrid world.
The authors argue that AgentSociety moves toward this highest level.
They claim the platform can simulate individuals, predict future behavior, and couple with real-time urban systems.
This is an ambitious framing.
The strongest part is the integration of agents, environment, and interventions.
The weakest part is that full real-world co-existence with society remains more of a vision than a completed validation.

## Discussion: Computational Social Science 2.0

The paper presents AgentSociety as part of a new paradigm in computational social science.
Traditional computational social science often focuses on explanation and prediction.
LLM-based silicon samples can reproduce some human survey behavior, but they often lack rich psychology, behavior, and environment.
Traditional ABM provides bottom-up simulation, but often uses simplified rule-based agents.
AgentSociety tries to combine the strengths of LLM agents and ABM.
It supports explanation, prediction, and intervention in the same platform.
The authors describe this as computational social science 2.0.
The central methodological claim is that realistic artificial societies can help researchers understand mechanisms by generating them.
The platform is therefore framed as a tool for both science and policy.

## Limitations

The simulator is ambitious, but many parts are still simplified.
The economic model does not fully represent labor markets, goods markets, unemployment, debt, investment, or firm competition.
The validity of social experiments depends on whether LLM agents actually reproduce human behavior in the relevant domain.
The system uses real-world data for the environment, but agent decision-making still depends on prompts, LLM biases, and model versions.
The platform’s performance is limited mainly by LLM API latency and cost.
The strongest validation is shown in selected experiments, but not every module is deeply benchmarked against real human data.
The social, economic, and urban modules are integrated, but their coupling may still be coarse.
Large-scale simulation can create plausible outputs that may be mistaken for validated predictions.
The paper would benefit from stronger ablation studies showing which modules are essential for each social result.

## Overall Interpretation

AgentSociety is one of the most ambitious LLM-agent social simulation platforms described in the current literature.
Its main contribution is not one specific social finding, but an integrated architecture for simulating artificial social life.
It combines mental states, memory, mobility, social interaction, economy, realistic environments, distributed execution, and social-science tools.
The paper is strongest as a system paper and research platform.
The exemplary experiments show that the system can reproduce plausible patterns in polarization, message spread, UBI response, hurricane mobility, and sustainability interventions.
The hurricane case is especially convincing because it compares simulated mobility trends with real data.
The system-performance results are also useful because they identify LLM calls as the main scalability bottleneck.
The main caution is that realism is uneven across modules and requires domain-specific validation.
AgentSociety is promising as a testbed, but not yet a fully validated replacement for real social experiments.

## Pros

* The platform is unusually comprehensive: it integrates psychological states, memory, mobility, social interaction, employment, consumption, and environmental feedback.

* The societal environment is a strong design choice because it separates objective constraints from LLM reasoning, reducing some hallucination risks.

* The system architecture is practical and scalable, using Ray, MQTT, PostgreSQL, and MLflow to support large-scale distributed simulation.

* The paper demonstrates diverse applications, including polarization, inflammatory-message spread, UBI, hurricane response, and sustainability interventions.

* The hurricane experiment is particularly specific and valuable because simulated mobility trends are compared with real-world movement data around landfall.

## Cons

* The economic module is still simplified: labor markets, goods markets, unemployment, debt, investment, and firm competition are not fully modeled.

* Many validation cases show plausible alignment, but the paper does not provide equally rigorous real-world benchmarking for every behavioral module.

* The simulator’s scalability is constrained by LLM API latency and cost, so very large or long simulations may require expensive private inference.

* The agent psychology is theoretically motivated, but the mapping from emotion, needs, and cognition modules to empirically valid human behavior remains uncertain.

* The platform’s broad scope makes it difficult to isolate which components actually cause each observed social result without more ablation studies.
